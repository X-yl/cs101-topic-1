The Impact of COVID-19 on Digital Safety

Aaron Mclean
October 21, 2021

Investment Scams


Types of Investment Scams

Investment scams are when people, through various methods, such as email, social media telephone, etc. imitate financial advisors and show investment opportunities which seem very desirable.

As the COVID-19 pandemic has had and still is having great effect on many aspects of our world, not least of which is the economy, which has seen moderate to severe decline in the past year.

The article by Vanderlaan(2020) states that this has increased the risk on the investor. This higher risk associated with investing in tandem with job uncertainty during the pandemic, means that the average investor is more vulnerable to fraud especially around retirement funds. The majority of these scams aim to take advantage of investors hysteria that has been caused due to the pandemic by promoting organisations that can “prevent, identify or cure covid-19”

Investment scams mostly affect those who are less experienced with investing who can’t tell when something is fake or when it’s too good to be true. They can be done via fake websites, social media posts etc.

How to protect yourself

The article by Vanderlaan(2020) goes on to explain ways in which the Massachusetts Securities Division have suggested for investors to protect themselves. These mostly include looking out for tell-tale details that identify a majority of these scams. They include:

  • Any relation to mitigating the effects of covid-19, as many of these new scams exploit the fear people have surrounding the pandemic. This is not a direct indication of it being a scam but should be enough to consider digging deeper about the investment opportunity
  • The promise of high return from either low input, or with irrelevant amounts of risk as this, unfortunately, is not realistic, as investing always has an inherent risk to it and high returns from these situations are opportunities that are few and far between.

Also, the article by Jarrad(2021) gives some further information from the French authorities DGCCRF and ACPR about how to avoid being taken advantage of. These include:

  • Where you got any investment information from, legitimate financial advisors will never send you information via social media or targeted emails, if you get an investment opportunity through either of these it is unlikely to be legitimate.
  • They also give the advice to not send any banking credentials or contact details online or via telephone, which should be fairly obvious.
  • Additionally they advise to conduct proper checks on the organisation that is providing you any investment opportunities, this includes: their website URL, company identity, proper licensing, wording of any correspondents, and the country they were established.
  • It is also possible to access the AMF’s database to see any blacklisted companies to avoid interacting with them. This is not a full-proof method however as it is possible the AMF doesn not know about any of this countries illegitimate actions
