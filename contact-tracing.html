COVID-19 & Privacy: Contact Tracing

Kyle Pereira
October 24, 2021

Contact tracing is a very important resource in disease control. It involves identifying possible contacts of infected people and subsequently collecting further information about these contacts. Traditional contact tracing was slow and labour intensive, requiring interviews and manually informing close contacts. The concept of digital contact tracing has existed since at least 2007 (Bahri, 2007), but only received widespread attention during the COVID-19 pandemic.

Many contact tracing apps have been criticised due to their privacy implications. A report published by RAND Corporation ranked COVID-19 apps based on multiple criteria, including transparency, anonymity and data management policies (Boudreaux et al., 2020), their report found that while some programs fully satisfied a majority of criteria, such as Australia’s COVIDSafe or the UK’s Privacy Protecting App which fully met 16/20 criteria, some applications like South Korea’s contact tracing program only fully met four criteria. Even so, some say that South Korea’s use of location data allowed them to avoid lockdowns. (Ryan, 2020)

Two possible methodologies are laid out for a contact tracing program in an article from Mayo Clinic Proceedings, (Kapa et al., 2020). In the more common scenario, a central authority is responsible for aggregating data and communicating with the user. This scenario leaves the user identifiable and does not protect privacy. In scenario 2, the data is de-identified before sending it to the central authority. The central authority then aggregates the anonymous data and publishes it. The user’s device then informs the user if their location data overlaps with any infected paths according to the published data.

Criticisms have been levelled against protocols like Pan-European Privacy-Preserving Proximity Tracing and BlueTrace for their use of a central server for processing logs (scenario 1 in this example).

Privacy recommendations for contact tracing apps include (Bengio et al., 2020)

  • Consent from users
  • Anonymisation of data
  • Decentralisation

It is critical to be aware of the technology behind digital contact tracing and advocate for privacy preserving implementations of the technology.
