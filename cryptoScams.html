Crypto Scams


Types of Crypto Scams

Due to the world’s economy being in a strange place, many people now believe that Crypto is the way forward for investors, with bitcoin rising more than $15000 during the pandemic according to the article by Kamalpour (2020).

Table adapted from : Table 1, A TAXONOMY OF 6 KINDS OF COVID-19 CRYPTOCURRENCY SCAMS (Xia et al., 2020).

This gives some examples of scams related to crypto currency, these are:

  • COVID-19 token scam, which is when a token is created that is claimed to donate to charity, but they are, as the paper so elegantly puts it, ““Pump-and-Dump” schemes”. They make money by convincing people to donate to this charity coin, and once the price is high enough, they cash out.
  • Crypto Malware, which is getting software onto a device, typically through a trojan, to block that device’s function or obtain personal data to “hold hostage” until a ransom of crypto is paid. There are other types of malware such as installing crypto miners onto a device, still via a trojan, and using that device to mine crypto without having to pay for the power as you typically would. The trojan has been disguised as COVID-19 related applications to take advantage of people trying to protect themselves.
  • COVID-19 giveaway scam, which is when a promise to return coins to a user based on how much they put in. These are typically spread via social media and other media sharing platforms by using copycat accounts of famous people or well known crypto investors, such as Bill Gates or Vitalink Buterin, or more rarely by hacking their accounts. The posts promoting these say things like “send bitcoin to 1Gatesk17u25gLEk4JNYMDTg8WkCmLpn4 and get double back” and would typically give a time limit to increase the chance of people making an uneducated, impulse decision.

(Kamalpour, 2020). Sites some large crytpo related scams that have happened in the past is May 2019, when $40 million worth of Bitcoin wallet passcodes were stolen, and a group from the Netherlands and the United Kingdom created aa fake exchange to gain access to almost $30 million worth of user bitcoin from over 10 countries. I mention these just to show that these scams can be executed with incredible finesse and are a very real risk to anybody involved or thing about getting involved in cryptocurrency trading.

How to protect yourself

So, unfortunately there isn’t a huge amount you can do to protect yourself from these types of scams outside of using a secure password and having two-factor identification on all crypto wallets but the rule “if it sounds too good to be true it probably is” is solid advice. However, there are some government efforts to manage crypto and hopefully reduce scams. (Kamalpour, 2020). lists some government legislation that various organisations have passed to help protect users online, this legislations is needed as cryptocurrency has only recently grown at such a massive rate and its taking Governments a bit of time to catch up. One of these is:

The FCA (Financial Conduct Authority) announced in October of 2020 that they were implementing a system to try and minimize damage caused by cryptocurrency scams on to retail consumers. This regulation aimed to minimize the damage without restricting people’s ability to use cryptocurrency. Many people have got misconceptions about this regulation as the rules are poorly understood but the aim is to target unregulated crypto assets to protect those less experienced with crypto.
