COVID-19 & Privacy: Rushed Adoption of Technology

Kyle Pereira
October 24, 2021

Many institutions were forced to switch to new platforms like Zoom in order to continue working. Zoom experienced huge growth during the pandemic, with approximately 470,000 customers, up 470% from previous years (“Zoom Video Communications Reports Fourth Quarter and Fiscal Year 2021 Financial Results - Zoom,” 2021). An article published in SAGE posits that institutions “rushed to obtain licenses for platforms that otherwise would have undergone more scrutiny from the data protection department” (Newlands et al., 2020).

Zoom has had several privacy vulnerabilities. In March 2020, it was discovered that the Zoom iOS app was sending analytics information to Facebook (Cox, 2020). There was a severe data security issue involving ‘Zoom-bombing’, where internet trolls join Zoom meetings and post hate or abuse, sometimes by simply guessing meeting IDs (Krebs, 2020), (Kunzelman, 2021).

The US Federal Trade Commission filed a complaint against Zoom, alleging that Zoom lied about its end-to-end encryption, stating that the encryption keys were held by Zoom (Brodkin, 2020). The FTC complaint also claimed that Zoom had misled users by saying meeting recordings were stored securely, however it was found that meetings were stored unencrypted for up to 60 days before being transferred to secure storage.

Institutions and businesses have also begun using surveillance software to track their students and employees. Universities began to use online proctoring services to surveil students during examinations (Harwell, 2020). Many students spoke up against the software, citing privacy violations and “inhumane examination conditions” (Chrysanthos, 2020; Harris, 2020). Professionals working from home have also noticed increased surveillance , with services like Sneek being deployed to take pictures of employees through their webcams every few minutes (Holmes, 2020).

An assessment of various work surveillance technologies ranked them on the basis of intrusiveness and reasonableness found that most people found most surveillance technologies unreasonable. The technologies found unreasonable by citizens include random photo captures, AI email software, key loggers and so on (Charbonneau and Doberstein, 2020). These technologies are mostly targeted towards remote working.

In conclusion, new technologies adopted due to the pandemic deserve more scrutiny about their privacy impact. A quick response to the pandemic should not have included adoption of new technologies without proper analysis of privacy and security risks.
